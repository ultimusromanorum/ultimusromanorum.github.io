Ultimus Romanorum

Morphology of Philosophical Texts

  1. Aphorism
  2. Autobiography
  3. Dialogue
  4. Encyclopedia
  5. Epistle
  6. Lesson
  7. Pamphlet
  8. Novel
  9. Essay
  10. Treatise

1. A form of writing that consists of pithy statements which assert something remarkable, and thus implicitly invites the reader to further investigate the matter independently. (e.g Guicciarddini's Ricordi, Leopardi's Pensieri, B. Gracian's Agudeza, G. C. Lichtenberg, La Bruyere, La Rochefoucauld, Pascal, de Chamfort, Desiderius Erasmus' Adagia, Schopenhauer's Parerga und Paralipomena)

2. A reconstruction of one's life for the purposes of investigating the personal phenomenon of intellectual formation. Other forms include prayers, confessions, and meditations. (e.g. Augustine's Confessiones, Bonaventure's Itinerarium mentis in Deum, Dante's Divina Commedia, Descartes' Discours sur la Methode)

3. A form of writing that tries to mimic the movement of philosophical conversations. (e.g. Platon, Augustine's Soliloquies, Anselm's Proslogion, Abelard's Sic et Non, Malebranche's Entretien d'un philosophe chretien, Berkeley's Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, Fontenelle's Dialogues of the Dead, Thomas Morgan's The Moral Philosopher, Hume's Dialogues concerning Natural Religion, Schelling's Bruno; lesser-known examples include Bruno's Italian dialogues, La Mothe Le Vayer's Dialogues Composed in Imitation of the Ancients, Cyrano de Bergerac's States and Empires of the Moon and the Sun, Vanini's Dei Mirabili Arcani della Natura)

4. A systematic exposition of philosophical knowledge. Some of the more distinctive forms of it include the medieval sententiae, summae, and disputationes. (e.g. Lombard's Sententiae, Aquinas' Summa Theologiae, Suárez's Disputationes, Vincenzo di Beauvais' Speculum maius, Lull's Arbor Scientiae, Bayle's Dictionnaire, Diderot's Encyclopedia, A. G. Baumgartner's Sciagraphia encyclopaediae philosophicae)

5. A friendly piece of correspondence directed to the exposition, and sometimes proof, of a particular point. (e.g. Platon, Epikuros, Seneca, Montesquieu's Letter persiane, Voltaire's Philosophical Letters)

5. A friendly piece of correspondence directed to the exposition, and sometimes proof, of a particular point. (e.g. Platon, Epikuros, Seneca, Montesquieu's Letter persiane, Voltaire's Philosophical Letters)

6. A piece of written composition intended as an elucidation of a particular topic or work of philosophy. (e.g. John of Salisbury's Metalogicon, the commentaries on Aristotle done by Albertus Magnus and St. Thomas Aquinas, biblical commentaries such as those on the Gospel of John done by Eriugena and Eckhart, the commentaries of Simplicius, etc.)

7. A booklet on some subject or question of current or temporary interest on which the writer desires to appeal to the public. (e.g. Plutarch's Adversus Colotem, Seneca's Apocolocyntosis, Tertullian's De ieiunio adversus psychicos, Milton's Areopagitica, Beza's De Jure Magistratuum, Hotman's Francogallia, Shelley's Necessity of Atheism)

8. A fictitious prose narrative which contains elements of a philosophical doctrine. (e.g. Voltaire's Candide, Diderot's Jacques le fataliste, More's Utopia, Schlegel's Lucinda, Hölderlin's Hyperion, Novalis, Goethe, Proust, H. James, R. Musil, Dostoevsky, Melville)

9. A compact piece of writing on a subject which manifests either in the form of a spontaneous meditation in the vein of Montaigne or in the form of a systematic work, such as those of Locke and Hume. (e.g. Montaigne, Bacon's Essays, Locke's An Essay Concerning Human Understanding, Johnson's The Tatler, Addison's The Spectator, Maimon's Essay [Versuch] on Transcendental Philosophy, Fichte's Critique of All Revelation, Herder, Goethe, Schlegel, Schopenhauer)

10. A book which contains a methodical discussion or exposition of a particular subject or a problem within it. (e.g. Aristotle's Poetica, Cicero's De Respublica, Machiavelli's Il principe, Botero's Della Ragion di Stato, Althusius' Politicus Methodice Digesta, Pufendorf's De Jure Naturae et Gentium, Hobbes' De Cive, Spinoza's Ethica, Locke's Two Treatises on Government, Berkeley's Treatise on the Principles of Human Knowledge, Hume's Treatise of Human Nature, Pomponazzi, Beccaria)
